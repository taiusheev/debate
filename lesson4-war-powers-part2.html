Module 02B | Mechanism Map

The Six Mechanisms: Status Quo vs. Reform

Skill objective: Identify which mechanism each case study activates and whether it favors Pro or Con. Map each status-quo congressional tool to its reform counterpart, identify the structural gap each pairing exposes, and assign the loophole risk to the correct side.
Hidden judge move: Name the mechanism, not just the case. "Libya happened" is not an argument. "Libya proves definitional evasion defeats the WPR clock" is an argument a judge can flow. Mechanism-level clash beats story-level clash. The team that names what structural gap a case proves — and explains why the other side's mechanism fails to close it — wins credibility with experienced judges even when the opponent has more cases.
Junior focus: There are six tools Congress uses right now to influence war. Pro says each one has a gap. Con says each one still works. Your job is to figure out which side is right for each tool — and to name the tool before you name the case.
Varsity focus: Each mechanism pairing exposes a distinct structural tension. The status-quo version relies on presidential cooperation or downstream politics; the Pro (reform) version relocates decision authority before hostilities begin. The loophole risk column is your clash map for every activity that follows.

Module 03 | Hook Activity

Institutional Capture: Permeability vs. Harm-Conversion

Skill objective: Distinguish which branch is more permeable to lobbying vs. which branch does more harm once captured. Run the two-step capture analysis independently: comparative permeability (ease of access) and comparative harm-conversion (severity of output once capture succeeds). Never conflate the two steps — they answer different questions.
Hidden judge move: Don't concede that "more lobbying" equals "more danger." The branch with more doors isn't automatically the branch with bigger weapons once capture lands. Force the judge to run both steps independently. Permeability determines likelihood of capture; harm-conversion determines magnitude per capture event. The winning side argues its metric — probability or magnitude — is the operative one for the ballot, rather than simply claiming its branch is lobbied more or less.
Frame the clash: the Military-Industrial Complex lobbies both branches. The question is which branch is more dangerous once capture succeeds. Lobbying volume is only half the story — the other half is what the captured branch does next.
Permeability (ease of access) vs. harm-conversion (severity of output once capture succeeds). Force each step independently. Pro argues executive harm-conversion is faster with fewer veto points. Con argues congressional permeability is higher volume, making capture more probable. Neither automatically wins — the clash is about which step does more analytical work for the ballot.
▲ Pro Proof Deck — Revolving Door (POGO 2018)
Claim: The executive branch converts MIC capture into military force faster and with fewer veto points, making it more dangerous than a captured Congress even if congressional permeability is higher.
"Top defense contractors hired 672 former government officials; approximately 91% later became registered lobbyists." — POGO (Project on Government Oversight), 2018
Statistic: 91% of 672 revolving-door hires became lobbyists — a direct pipeline from executive decision-making centers to contractor influence networks.
Warrant: A captured President can deploy forces unilaterally within hours. A captured Congress still faces committee procedures, floor votes, and the other chamber — meaning executive capture has faster harm-conversion even if congressional permeability is higher in volume.
Deploy line (Junior)
"Even if Congress gets lobbied more, one captured President can move troops with no vote. That's faster harm-conversion — and faster means worse."
Deploy line (Varsity)
"The POGO data proves the revolving door funnels influence into the executive. Ackerman 2010 shows executive capture converts into force with fewer veto points — making executive permeability more dangerous even at lower volume."
▼ Con Proof Deck — FARA / Quincy Data
Claim: Congress is the primary target of foreign and defense lobbying, making congressional war authority more susceptible to capture than presidential authority — higher volume means higher probability of capture landing.
"More than half of reported foreign-agent political activities targeted Congress, totaling nearly 70,000 contacts with House and Senate offices." — Quincy Institute / FARA data, 2022-23
Statistic: 70,000 lobbying contacts with Congress from foreign agents alone in 2022-23 — more than half of all reported foreign-agent political activity.
Warrant: If the resolution routes final war authority through Congress, it routes that authority through the branch with the highest volume of foreign and defense lobbying — increasing the probability of capture, not reducing it. Volume matters because sufficient volume eventually succeeds even with more veto points.
Deploy line (Junior)
"Shifting power to Congress means shifting power to the branch lobbyists already target 70,000 times a year. More doors means more chances for capture to land."
Deploy line (Varsity)
"The FARA data quantifies congressional permeability at 70,000 foreign-agent contacts. Posner and Vermeule argue formal rules don't bind anyway — real constraints are political. The plan routes force authority through the most lobbied institution and adds only paper constraints."
Two-step framework reminder: These two proof decks fight on different dimensions. Pro says: even if Congress is lobbied more, executive harm-conversion is faster. Con says: lobbying volume matters because it shows which branch is likelier to be captured in the first place. You need to explain which step matters more — probability or magnitude per event. The two-step capture framework forces each side to compete on a different axis. Pro dominates on harm-conversion speed and veto-point count (magnitude per event). Con dominates on permeability volume (probability of capture). The resolution to the clash requires a weighing argument: is probability of capture (Con's metric) or severity once captured (Pro's metric) the operative standard for the ballot?
▲ Pro Response Cards

Con says "Congress is lobbied most"

  • Rebuttal: Lobbying volume does not equal harm-conversion speed. More doors doesn't mean bigger weapons — a 70,000-contact Congress still takes weeks to authorize force.
  • Crossfire trap: "If Congress is so permeable, why does your plan route all war authority through the branch lobbyists hit 70,000 times a year?"
  • Weighing: Magnitude — one captured-executive strike vs. a slow lobbied-Congress authorization process with multiple veto points that may prevent the worst outcome.
▼ Con Response Cards

Pro says "executive converts capture faster"

  • Rebuttal: Political accountability still constrains Presidents — elections, public opinion, and media scrutiny all create real costs that slow harm-conversion.
  • Crossfire trap: "Name one time Congress authorized a war it didn't want under foreign lobbying pressure — give a specific example."
  • Weighing: Probability — Congress is lobbied at much higher volume, making capture more likely even if each individual capture event converts more slowly.
Write your best capture line for either side — name the branch, the lobbying evidence, and what capture produces.Write a structured two-step capture argument: Step 1 (comparative permeability claim with citation), Step 2 (harm-conversion claim with veto-point count), Step 3 (weighing metric that resolves it).

Module 04 | Activity 1

Informal Shaping vs. First-Move Problem

Skill objective: Evaluate whether status-quo congressional signals provide binding threshold control or only downstream influence over a decision the executive has already made. Analytically separate informal shaping (political costs without formal authorization requirement) from threshold control (binding ex ante authorization gate). Identify why the first-move problem renders informal shaping structurally insufficient — the executive can absorb political costs post-hoc when national security interests are at stake.
Hidden judge move: Con will claim informal shaping "already works." Pro must prove that shaping is not the same as threshold control — because the executive still holds the first move and can redefine the trigger when shaping fails. Pro's core analytical move: informal shaping creates political costs but does not create binding threshold control. The structural difference is who holds the first move. Con answers with Syria 2013 (informal shaping succeeded). Pro answers with Libya 2011 (formal resistance ignored). The clash resolves on: which is the structural norm and which is the exception — and what structural incentive determines which happens in the modal case?
Syria 2013 — Congress pushed back and Obama didn't strike. Does that prove Pro is unnecessary, or does it prove the system was lucky? Think about what would have happened if the administration had decided to strike despite the opposition — just like in Libya.
Informal shaping creates real political costs for force but does not recreate a binding ex ante authorization requirement. The first-move problem: the executive can absorb political costs post-hoc when national security interests are at stake. Pro must prove Libya (formal resistance ignored) is the structural norm; Con must prove Syria (informal shaping succeeded) is the norm. Neither side has exhaustive data — the clash is about structural incentives.
▲ Pro — Libya 2011: Definitional Evasion Defeats Formal Resistance
Claim: Informal congressional resistance fails to prevent unauthorized force because the executive can redefine the legal trigger to avoid the formal check — a structural escape hatch that informal shaping cannot close.
"The House of Representatives rejected authorization for the Libya operation by a vote of 123–295, but the operation continued because the administration argued the airstrikes and drone strikes did not constitute 'hostilities' under the War Powers Resolution." — House vote on Libya authorization, 2011
Statistic: 123–295 House vote rejecting authorization; zero operational change as a result. The operation continued for 7+ months.
Warrant: Libya 2011 proves that congressional resistance — even a formal floor vote — cannot stop an executive that controls the definitional trigger. Without mandatory pre-authorization, the President defines when the check applies. The first-move problem is structural, not incidental.
Deploy line (Junior)
"Congress voted 123-295 against Libya — and the bombs kept falling. That's not informal shaping working; that's definitional evasion making the check meaningless even when Congress acts formally."
Deploy line (Varsity)
"Libya 2011 proves the first-move problem's structural limit. The administration claimed the WPR clock never started because airstrikes weren't 'hostilities.' Without mandatory pre-authorization, the executive always holds the definitional key — informal or formal congressional resistance cannot reconstruct that key."
▼ Con — Syria 2013 + Howell & Pevehouse: Shaping Already Works
Claim: Congressional signaling already constrains major force decisions without a formal pre-authorization rule — the status quo worked in Syria 2013, and Howell & Pevehouse quantify that congressional composition regularly shapes force decisions.
"Congressional composition significantly predicts major uses of force — suggesting that political constraints already operate meaningfully on presidential force decisions." — Howell & Pevehouse, 2007, "While Dangers Gather"
Case: Syria 2013: Congressional opposition persuaded the Obama administration to pull back from a planned strike before launch. No new law required it — political reality did the work.
Warrant: If informal shaping already stopped the Syria strike, Pro's plan adds procedural friction without demonstrating unique solvency. Howell & Pevehouse show that congressional composition already influences major force decisions — not just in individual cases but systematically across the data.
Deploy line (Junior)
"Syria 2013 happened with no new rule. Congress pushed back and the strike was canceled. The status quo already does what Pro claims only its plan can achieve."
Deploy line (Varsity)
"Howell and Pevehouse quantify that congressional composition already predicts major force decisions. Pro must explain why Libya-style definitional evasion is the structural norm rather than the exception before claiming the plan adds unique threshold value beyond what informal shaping already delivers."
Key distinction — Syria vs. Libya: Pro must distinguish Libya (formal resistance ignored and definitional escape used) from Syria (informal shaping worked). The key question: is Syria the rule or the exception? If Syria is reproducible, Con wins. If Libya represents the structural incentive when stakes are high, Pro wins. The clash resolves on comparative probability and structural incentive. Pro needs the Libya pattern to be structural — the executive always has definitional escape available when it chooses to use it. Con needs Syria to be reproducible — political costs reliably deter the executive from using definitional escape in high-stakes situations. The modal-case argument determines which structural incentive dominates.
▲ Pro Response Cards

Con says "Syria proves informal shaping works"

  • Rebuttal: Syria was informal and luck-dependent; Libya shows definitional evasion makes formal checks meaningless when the executive decides to move. Same president, same era — different outcome.
  • Crossfire trap: "If informal shaping works, why did Libya happen with the same administration one year earlier? What made Syria different, and can you guarantee that difference in the next case?"
  • Weighing: Reversibility — a failed informal signal leaves a war running with no structural remedy. Syria's luck doesn't undo Libya's 7-month unauthorized campaign.
▼ Con Response Cards

Pro says "Libya proves the plan is needed"

  • Rebuttal: Libya was a specific operation with specific evasion tactics — not a structural norm. Syria shows the political system corrects itself when stakes and opposition intensity are high enough.
  • Crossfire trap: "Does your mandatory pre-authorization plan prevent definitional evasion, or just add a new trigger term — like 'emergency' — that can also be redefined the same way 'hostilities' was?"
  • Weighing: Probability — Howell & Pevehouse show congressional composition regularly matters. How often does Libya-style evasion occur vs. Syria-style shaping success?
Is Syria the rule or the exception? Write 2 sentences explaining your answer.Write a structured argument for why Libya or Syria represents the structural norm — include a mechanism warrant (why the structural incentive favors your case) and a weighing claim (why your case outweighs the opponent's).
